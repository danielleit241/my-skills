Question 01

What is Design Thinking?

A human-centered problem-solving approach — focused on deeply understanding user needs, reframing problems, and generating creative solutions through rapid, iterative experimentation. Not a rigid process — but a flexible mindset.
🧭
Human-centered
Starts from the real needs of users — not from technology assumptions or team biases. Empathy is the foundation.
🔄
Iterative
Test early, fail fast, learn continuously. Each loop refines the team's understanding of the actual problem.
🤝
Collaborative
Combines diverse perspectives: engineering, design, business, end users — cross-functional thinking in one room.
⚡
Action-oriented
Turn ideas into tangible prototypes fast. "Show, don't tell" — bias toward action over endless analysis.

2

3

Unconstrained brainstorming — diverge first, converge later
Crazy 8 · Mind Map
4

Build fast, build cheap — good enough to learn from, not to ship
Paper · Wireframe · PoC
5

Put prototypes in front of real users — observe, capture, iterate
Usability Test · A/B

⟳  Non-linear — insights from Test can send the team back to Empathize or Define. Iteration is a feature, not a failure.

Question 02

Design Thinking in
Requirement Engineering

Traditional RE asks: "What does the system need to do?"
Design Thinking shifts the question to: "What do users actually need — and why?" Then the system gets defined.
Collecting requirements from stakeholders → Uncovering hidden needs through observation
Writing hundred-page SRS documents → Lightweight prototypes to validate early
Modeling actors and use cases → Empathy mapping + User journey
Freezing requirements at project start → Iterative refinement across sprints

In Practice

4 DT Techniques in Requirement Engineering

🗺 Empathy Mapping

4 quadrants: Says / Thinks / Does / Feels — surfaces contradictions between what users say and what they actually do.

Ex: User says "I need better reports" — they actually just need a fast Excel export.
→ Empathize stage
💡 HMW Questions

"How Might We…" reframes problems as design opportunities — expands the solution space instead of prematurely narrowing it.

Ex: "HMW help accountants stop copy-pasting data manually every day?"
→ Define stage
📋 User Story Mapping

Lay out the user journey horizontally, tasks vertically — see the backlog by delivered value, not just effort.

Ex: Discovered 3 independent tasks that could release MVP 2 sprints earlier.
→ Define + Ideate
📄 Paper Prototyping

Validate requirements with hand-drawn sketches before writing code — catch UX and flow errors at near-zero cost.

Ex: A 30-min sketch session caught a login flow confusion → saved 2 sprints of refactoring.
→ Prototype + Test

Question 03

DT in Intensive Software
Development Projects

What is Intensive Software Development?

Projects with tight deadlines, large teams, constantly shifting scope, and pressure from all directions. This environment leads many teams to skip DT — and that is the classic mistake. DT is not extra work — it's a different way of thinking that ensures you build the right thing from the start.

Fail fast, learn faster: Small prototypes expose mistakes in week 1, not sprint 10. The cost to fix issues early is up to 100× lower than fixing them late.
Shared understanding beats long specs: A 2-hour DT workshop creates better alignment than a 50-page requirements document — everyone sees the same problem.
User-centered backlog: Every user story grounded in real insight — reduces "build the wrong thing" mistakes that waste future sprints on rework.
Creative confidence: DT practice builds a culture of "try instead of overthink" — perfectly suited to the pace of intensive projects.

Sprint Review
End of sprint

After sprint
"What did we learn about users?" not just process

DT doesn't replace Agile — it amplifies the quality of each sprint's input by ensuring the team always solves the right problem for the right users.

Assessment

Benefits & Real-World Challenges

✦ Measurable Benefits
  • ✓Early risk detection — week-1 prototypes replace month-1 specs; problems surface before a line of code is written.
  • ✓Dramatically less rework — continuous validation ensures the team builds what's actually needed, not what's assumed.
  • ✓Faster team alignment — shared empathy creates a common language across dev, BA, and business stakeholders.
  • ✓Innovation culture — DT mindset builds a culture of experimentation and reduces fear of failure within teams.
◆ Practical Challenges
  • !Upfront time cost — Empathize and Define phases require investment before any tangible output appears.
  • !Hard to scale — DT workshops work best with teams of 5–9; 20+ people need an experienced facilitator.
  • !Mindset shift takes time — "solution-first" culture often resists "problem-first" thinking at the start.
  • !Deadline pressure — under squeeze, DT activities are first to get cut — requires strong management sponsorship.
💡
When to apply DT? — Most effective when the problem is ambiguous, users don't yet know what they need, or the current solution has high friction. Less suited for well-defined technical tasks with stable, clear requirements.
10 / 12
Takeaways

3 things to remember from today's open talk

01
DT is a mindset, not a process
You don't need to follow all 5 steps. What matters is starting from the user, not from the solution. Even spending 30 minutes observing users before coding is a DT mindset in action.
02
DT and Agile complement each other
DT answers "build the right thing" — Agile answers "build the thing right". Sprint 0 + DT activities is the most effective integration point for intensive projects.
03
Early prototypes save the most
The cost of fixing bugs grows exponentially over time. A 30-minute paper prototype saves more than 2 sprints of refactoring. Fail fast, learn faster — not a motto, a strategy.
