**"Requirement Engineering and the Role of Design Thinking"**
Focusing on technical processes, problem-solving mechanisms, and practical implementation stages from the source material:

### 1. Detailed Breakdown of Traditional Requirements Engineering (RE)

The research clarifies that Requirements Engineering is not a single step but a sequence of strictly structured activities within the traditional waterfall model. These stages include:

- **Requirements Definition:** The foundational stage for establishing the system's scope and boundaries through elicitation techniques.
- **Interviews:** A tool to discover facts and opinions from users while eliminating misunderstandings. This takes two forms: closed interviews (predefined questions) and open interviews (free discussion). The primary challenge lies in processing massive amounts of qualitative data and handling conflicting information from different stakeholders.
- **Use Cases:** Focused on describing the flow of interaction between external actors and the system to explain functional needs from an early stage.
- **Social Observation and Analysis:** Engineers directly immerse themselves in the users' work environment to build knowledge of their operational reality.
- **Focus Groups:** Bringing together 4 to 9 individuals from various disciplines for free discussion regarding prototype features, helping to prioritize needs.
- **Brainstorming:** Divided into an ideation phase (non-critical) and an evaluation phase to select the most feasible ideas.
- **Prototyping:** Building early versions of the system to gather further information on user expectations.
- **Requirements Analysis:** Delving deep into business logic to verify whether gathered requirements are comprehensive, feasible, and consistent.
- **Requirements Documentation:** Transforming requirements into written text to communicate between stakeholders and the development team.
- **Requirements Validation:** Using test scenarios or prototypes to check if the documentation accurately describes and is acceptable to user needs.
- **Requirements Management:** Tracking all changes and ensuring adjustments align with business needs throughout the development lifecycle.

### 2. The Shift to Agile RE and Its Gaps

Agile emerged to increase flexibility through frameworks such as **Scrum**, **Kanban**, or **Extreme Programming**.

- **Core Values:** Prioritizing working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a plan.
- **Operational Mechanism:** Requirements are listed in a backlog and discussed periodically every two weeks (sprint) to clarify and prioritize implementation.
- **Challenges:** Despite increased flexibility, failure rates for Agile projects tend to rise due to ambiguity in executing RE processes and an over-reliance on high-skilled individuals.

### 3. Design Thinking (DT) - The "Sweet Spot" in Requirements Management

DT is not just a process but a structured methodology for solving complex problems in a volatile world.

- **The "Sweet Spot" Concept:** In system design, the goal is to find a balance that provides enough requirement information while maintaining a reasonable degree of ambiguity, allowing the development team space for exploration and creativity.
- **Solving Customer Problems:** DT allows for user interview planning from the very beginning, overcoming the difficulty of customer access that often slows down requirement clarification in Agile.
- **Prioritizing Usability:** While user stories often focus solely on software features, DT forces the team to emphasize non-functional requirements like security and, crucially, usability.
- **Knowledge Distribution:** DT promotes the sharing of knowledge (often siloed within the minds of experts) across the entire team through multidisciplinary teamwork.
- **Problem Reframing:** DT allows for the iterative definition of problems and solutions to ensure the best User Experience (UX) is delivered.

### 4. Implementation Details: Financial Project Case Study

The research empirically tested the use of "Up-front DT" in a project at a large organization:

- **Context:** A project building a new financial analysis tool involving approximately 20 people.
- **Workshop Progression (1 week):**
  - **Empathy:** Conducting interviews and shadowing existing users in their actual environment.
  - **Problem Definition:** The team collaboratively builds a clearly stated problem statement.
  - **Ideation:** Using the **"How-might we"** method to stimulate creativity and generate multiple potential solutions.
  - **Prototyping and Testing:** Selecting the best solution to build a digital prototype and allowing users to run trials.
  - **Iteration:** Based on lessons from testing, the team continuously adjusts the prototype until user acceptance is reached.
- **Preliminary Results:**
  - **Human Desirability:** The user acceptance rate for the first release was higher than average.
  - **Technological Feasibility:** The company selected the correct technology after the appropriate solution was identified, avoiding wasted investment in multiple previous technologies.
  - **Business Viability:** Recorded lower operational costs compared to similar projects that did not apply DT from the start.

**In-depth Conclusion:** Integrating Design Thinking into Requirements Engineering helps transform "wicked problems" into approachable ones, creating a synergistic relationship that improves the entire development process and the quality of the final product.
