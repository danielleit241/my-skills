**"A systematic review of design thinking implementations in higher engineering education" (2026)** by Yuwei Deng, Claire Lucas, and Wei Liu.
Below is a detailed interpretation, combining translation and in-depth analysis of the key arguments from the source material:

### 1. Context and the Rise of Design Thinking (DT) in Engineering

The study begins by identifying DT as a human-centered problem-solving methodology that emphasizes understanding user needs, iterative prototyping, and creative problem-solving.

- **Mindset Shift:** In traditional engineering education, the focus is often on "science thinking"—concentrating on the optimization and analysis of well-defined problems. However, professional reality requires engineers to confront "ambiguous, open-ended problems." DT emerges as a critical pedagogical strategy to equip students with the ability to navigate complex real-world challenges.
- **Research Gap:** Although DT is widely adopted, practices remain fragmented due to variations in implementation and organizational barriers. This review analyzes 76 empirical studies from 2003 to 2023 to provide evidence-based guidance.

### 2. Defining Core Concepts: Design and Thinking

The research clarifies foundational terms to establish the analytical scope:

- **Design:** Rather than merely investigating existing reality (as science does), design is the creation of a new reality according to human intent. Design problems are characterized as "open," requiring synthesis rather than pure analysis.
- **Thinking:** There are three main theoretical perspectives:
  - **Cognitivist:** Thinking as internal mental processes, reasoning strategies, and problem-solving.
  - **Pragmatist:** Engineers "think through technology"—thinking occurs through interaction with materials and tools.
  - **Post-cognitivist:** Thinking is distributed across the mind, body, tools, and social context.

### 3. The HOW–WHAT–WHY Analytical Framework

The study utilizes this framework to systematize knowledge:

#### WHAT: Most Common Definitions and Models

The research found convergence around three primary sources of definition:

1.  **Tim Brown (IDEO):** DT is an innovation process that integrates human needs, technological feasibility, and business viability (accounted for 60% of studies).
2.  **Dym et al. (2005):** Emphasizes practical competencies such as managing ambiguity, divergent-convergent thinking, and systems thinking (30%).
3.  **Buchanan (1992):** Views DT as an empirical counterpoint to traditional methodologies, helping to solve "wicked problems."

Regarding process models, the **Stanford d.school 5-stage model** is the most popular: Empathize – Define – Ideate – Prototype – Test. Some studies customized this model by adding steps like "implementation" or integrating it with frameworks such as CDIO and PBL (Project-Based Learning).

#### HOW: Implementation Strategies and Assessment

- **Integration Levels:** DT is primarily taught at the **course level** (semester-long or short-term) and through **experiential projects** (capstone projects, hackathons).
- **Innovation Ecosystems:** Spaces such as Makerspaces, multidisciplinary labs, and digital platforms play a vital role in supporting student experimentation and collaboration.
- **Collaboration:** DT typically requires diverse teamwork environments, including intra-disciplinary, interdisciplinary (with business or arts students), and collaboration with industry experts.
- **Competency Assessment:** There is a diversity of assessment methods, but the most common are surveys (41%) and rubric-based design projects (36%). Interviews and reflective journals are also used to capture cognitive and emotional aspects. Notably, direct observation of behavior remains rare (only 4%).

#### WHY: Benefits for Stakeholders

- **For Students:** DT facilitates deeper acquisition of technical knowledge through application contexts, while addressing emotional needs such as increasing confidence, motivation, and autonomy.
- **For Teachers:** DT serves as a modeling tool for training and developing innovative teaching methods.
- **For Society:** DT promotes **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** by creating environmentally and socially responsible solutions. It also plays a role in **social justice**, helping to bridge the gender gap and support underrepresented groups in engineering.

### 4. Conclusions and Recommendations for the Future

- **Current State:** DT has become a significant pedagogical movement, but conceptual ambiguity persists as it is simultaneously understood as a process, a mindset, and a set of competencies.
- **Recommendations:**
  - Focus on longitudinal studies to evaluate the impact of DT on engineers' careers after graduation.
  - Develop a detailed and standardized syllabus to avoid fragmentation in implementation.
  - Place greater emphasis on faculty training and support for underrepresented student groups (such as women and people with disabilities).
  - The future of DT may integrate with **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** to personalize learning and provide real-time feedback during design cycles.

This study affirms that DT is not just a supplementary tool but a necessary **reform of engineering education** to produce engineers capable of creative thinking and solving the complex problems of the 21st century.
